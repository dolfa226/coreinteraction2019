International Symbol of Access
  • Head is not independent of body
  • Limbs are sharp edges, not softly rounded
  • Person in the International Symbol of Access is drawn like “like an object compared to the people shown standing and walking with briefcases or children”
    • Timeline

    1968
    Danish design student Susanne Koefoed created the original version of the International Symbol of Access icon without a head

    later added head and incorporated into International Organization for Standardization’s collection of symbols for equipment

    2010
    Sara Hendren collected icons

    2011
    Brian Glenney made their own icon, made stickers and spread them throughout Boston. Icon was made into a clear-backed square sticker that would be place on top of the original to show the old image and the new one simultaneously

    Tim Ferguson-Sauder joined the team and then released as public domain

    “I don’t mind the older symbol “but I definitely think this is a step, roll if you will, in the right direction… To me, the more active look of the ‘revamped’ icon better represents the freedom and equality that accessibility truly brings.” It might not represent him absolutely—Mort is a power-chair user—but he appreciates the meaning behind it, and acknowledges that “it’s impossible to capture the diverse experiences and needs of the disabled community with a singular design.”

    Not everyone is a wheelchair athlete. What about people who don’t push their chairs with their own arms?
    "The arm pushing a chair is symbolic—as all icons are symbols, not literal representations.”

    I identify as disabled, but I don’t use a chair. Why should that symbol speak for all kinds of accessibility?
    The icon is a symbol for the larger topic of disabilities
    A highly recognizable symbol allows for deeper understanding

    Why is this important?

  • importance: starting a conversation
  • it is impossible to cater to everyone
  • good design is about understanding the context of the situation you’re designing for
  • but if we at least aim for inclusivity in our designs, we will create betters design because we’re expanding and thinking in different ways
